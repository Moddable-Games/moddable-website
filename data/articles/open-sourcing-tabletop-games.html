Should board games be open source? It's a slightly uncomfortable question for an industry built on intellectual property protection — but it's one of the most important questions facing tabletop gaming today.

Players already modify games extensively. They rebalance mechanics, create expansions, build digital versions, and share house rules across forums and Discord servers. The modification is happening regardless of whether publishers acknowledge it. The question is not if players will shape games — it's whether the industry structures itself to embrace that reality.

So what does "open-source" even mean for tabletop games?

In software, open-source means access to the code that makes a product work. For tabletop games, the equivalent involves a different set of layers:

  • Access to master source files for editing gameplay mechanics and rules
  • Community-owned artwork with multiple themes and visual identities
  • Digital sandboxes for online play and remote testing
  • Access to transparent global manufacturing partnerships

Each layer represents a different kind of openness. A publisher might open their rules but keep artwork proprietary. They might release digital assets but retain manufacturing exclusivity. Structured openness is not all-or-nothing — it's a spectrum.

The fear and the opportunity.

Designers, publishers, and artists have legitimate concerns. If anyone can modify and redistribute a game, what protects the creators who invested years of work? What prevents dilution of a brand? What happens to revenue?

But open-source models in other industries haven't eliminated value — they've shifted where value lives. Red Hat built a multi-billion-dollar business on free software. WordPress powers 40% of the web while remaining open-source. The value moved from controlling the artifact to cultivating the ecosystem.

"Open-source models haven't eliminated value. They've shifted where value lives."

Enduring ecosystems over isolated hits.

The next generation of players has grown up with modded video games, remix culture, and collaborative platforms. They expect participation, not just consumption. A publisher who builds an ecosystem — tools, communities, shared standards — will outlast one who defends a single product behind walls.

This doesn't mean every game should be fully open on day one. But it does mean the industry needs new models for balancing openness with sustainability. Selective openness. Tiered access. Community governance. Revenue sharing.

What we believe.

The games that endure are the ones that communities invest in, and communities invest in things they can shape.

The question is not whether players will shape games. It's whether we acknowledge that they already do.
