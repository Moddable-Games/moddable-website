Conquering the Constrains of Chess.

Going beyond the game — if chess was designed today, how would it differ?

▲ AUTHOR
Mark Smalley
CEO & Founder
▲ SHARE

If chess was designed today, how would the game differ? It's a question that has occupied a decade of thinking — and it starts with understanding where deterministic strategy games came from.

Checkers appeared approximately 5,000 years ago. Go followed around 4,000 years ago. Chess arrived roughly 1,500 years ago. Each represented a leap in strategic complexity, but all three share a common constraint: they are games for exactly two players, played on a static board.

The problem with predictability.

Chess's opening theory is so deeply mapped that grandmasters routinely play 15-20 moves from memory before original thought begins. The static 8x8 grid with fixed starting positions means the game's possibility space, while enormous, is ultimately finite and increasingly charted.

This is not a flaw — it's a feature that has sustained the game for centuries. But it does raise the question: what would a modern designer do differently?

Hexagons and infinite setups.

The proposed solution begins with the board itself. Replace squares with hexagons. Introduce diverse biomes — terrain types that modify movement and engagement rules. Suddenly, every game starts from a unique configuration.

A hexagonal grid also removes the two-player constraint. With six natural axes instead of four, the geometry supports three, four, or six players without awkward asymmetry. The board becomes a landscape rather than a battlefield.

"If you change the board, you change the game. If you make the board modular, you make the game infinite."

Accessibility as a design principle.

Modern board games have trended toward increasing complexity and cost. A single game can require hundreds of miniatures, a 60-page rulebook, and a four-figure investment. Chess's genius was always its accessibility: 32 pieces, 64 squares, rules you can teach in ten minutes.

Any modern reimagining must honour that accessibility while expanding the strategic depth. Fewer components, more emergent complexity. Simple rules, infinite expression.
