Making a Masterpiece by Modification.

The checkered history of chess — from ancient India to the modern modding table.

▲ AUTHOR
Kevin Chand
Chief Playtester
▲ SHARE

Chess is not one game. It is a family of games — a lineage stretching back 1,500 years across continents, cultures, and countless modifications. What we call "chess" today is simply the variant that stuck.

The story begins in 6th-century India with Chaturanga — a four-player war simulation played on an 8x8 grid called an ashtapada. The pieces represented infantry, cavalry, elephants, and chariots. The rules were fluid, regional, and constantly evolving.

The Silk Road transmissions.

As the game travelled westward along the Silk Road, it transformed. In Persia it became Shatranj, shedding two players and gaining the strategic depth of alternating turns. The Arabs refined it further, developing the first recorded opening theory and endgame studies.

Eastward, the game evolved into Xiangqi (Chinese Chess), which introduced mechanics still unseen in the Western game: a river barrier dividing the board, restricted palace zones for generals, and cannons that must leap over an intervening piece to capture.

Europe and the modern game.

Chess arrived in Europe between the 10th and 15th centuries, and the transformation was dramatic. The slow-moving fers (counsellor) became the all-powerful Queen. The elephant became the Bishop. The game accelerated — what had been a grinding siege became a rapid tactical battle.

This was, in essence, a mod. The European players took an existing game, kept the grid and the turn structure, and rewrote the piece movement rules to suit their cultural preferences for speed and decisiveness.

Historical variants.

  • Tamerlane Chess — A 112-square board with unique pieces including giraffes, war engines, and a piece that moves as a combination of knight and rook.
  • Xiangqi — River barriers and cannons make for a more direct, tactical game with faster resolution.
  • Shogi — Captured pieces can be re-deployed, ensuring games never simplify and drawless endgames.
  • Courier Chess — A 12x8 medieval variant popular for centuries before the modern queen was introduced.

The modern modding era.

Today, chess modifications continue to proliferate. 3D Chess adds a vertical dimension. Hexagonal Chess (Glinski's variant) replaces the square grid entirely. Alice Chess uses two boards with pieces phasing between them. Bughouse Chess turns the game into a team sport with piece trading between boards.

"Chess has always been modified. The version we play today is simply the mod that won."

Notable figures throughout history understood this. Napoleon Bonaparte played chess obsessively during his campaigns. Benjamin Franklin wrote The Morals of Chess, promoting the game for teaching "foresight, caution and perseverance." Einstein was a devoted player who saw the game as a model for scientific thinking.
