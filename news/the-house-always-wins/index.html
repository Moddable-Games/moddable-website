The House Always Wins!

House rules that became canon — and what they tell us about the future of game design.

▲ AUTHOR
Kevin Chand
Chief Playtester
▲ SHARE

Anyone who has rolled dice on a board has said at least once: “We don’t play it that way.” House rules are often dismissed as casual tweaks. Shortcuts for impatient players or patches for perceived flaws. Sometimes though, house rules do something much more interesting. Sometimes, they become canon.

No matter how hard some publishers might try, rulebooks are not sacred scripture. They are a snapshot of a designer’s intent at a moment frozen in time. It is on the table where reality intervenes. Players discover strategies that stall momentum, or economic assumptions that unnecessarily inflate time. Some find eliminations discourage play or downtime that depletes a desire to replay. Yet in households in every nation all over the world, players adjust.

Rules that rewrote themselves.

What’s fascinating is not that house rules exist; it’s that some become widespread enough to redefine a game entirely. Consider en passant in Chess — first introduced as a house rule over 500 years ago and eventually standardised internationally 200 years later. Some people still believe it’s merely a house rule and rage quit when used against them.

Or take Monopoly, a game with more unofficial variants than official editions. The free parking jackpot. Getting paid double for landing on Go. Auctioning houses not bought when landed on — which is, ironically, an actual official rule that most players mistakenly believe is a house rule.

Why does this matter?

House rules reveal three important truths. Players are active participants in design. Communities will optimise for fun over purity. And the final version of the game may not be the one printed.

If players are rewriting your rules, it implies they’re deeply engaged with your system. In Dungeons & Dragons, homebrew content isn’t fringe behaviour; it’s foundational culture. Entire campaign settings, character classes, mechanics and alternate systems were born at home tables before influencing official releases.

Versioning the tabletop.

What if publishers actively tracked and encouraged house rules? In a digital era, rules no longer need to be static. They can be versioned, tested, forked and merged. The open-source software world solved this decades ago. Tabletop games may be next — so join our community to discuss this and other topics.
